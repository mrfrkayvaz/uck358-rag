The figure presents two graphs comparing the yaw rate response ($\Delta \beta$, in degrees) of the F-100A aircraft with small and large tail configurations at different flight conditions. 

### Left Graph: F-100A Small Tail
- **Variables**: 
  - **Horizontal Axis**: $p_0$ (yaw rate, in degrees/second), ranging from -150 to 0 to +50.
  - **Vertical Axis**: $\Delta \beta$ (change in sideslip angle, in degrees), ranging from -20 to 0.
- **Curves**: 
  - The solid line represents steady-state conditions, while the dashed line represents a dynamic model with five degrees of freedom during a 360° roll.
- **Insight**: The small tail configuration shows a significant negative change in sideslip angle as yaw rate increases, indicating a tendency for increased sideslip that could affect stability.

### Right Graph: F-100A Large Tail
- **Variables**: 
  - **Horizontal Axis**: $p_c$ (yaw rate, in degrees/second), ranging from -200 to +200.
  - **Vertical Axis**: $\Delta \beta$ (change in sideslip angle, in degrees), starting from 0 and going up to 20.
- **Curves**: 
  - Similar to the left graph, it shows both steady-state and dynamic responses.
- **Insight**: The large tail configuration exhibits a more stable response, with a positive change in sideslip angle at higher yaw rates, suggesting better control and stability in flight.

### Key Engineering Insight
The comparison highlights how tail size affects yaw stability and control characteristics. The small tail configuration may lead to adverse yaw characteristics, while the large tail configuration provides enhanced stability, crucial for aircraft design and performance analysis. Understanding these dynamics is essential for ensuring safe and effective aircraft operation under various flight conditions.
