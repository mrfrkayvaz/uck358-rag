This figure illustrates the aerodynamic analysis of rudder effectiveness on an aircraft, focusing on full-span and partial-span rudders. It highlights the relationship between rudder dimensions, angles of attack, and their impact on the aircraft's control surfaces.

### Key Components:
1. **Rudder Types**:
   - **Full-span Rudder**: Shown at the top, with a relative wind angle of 45°.
   - **Partial-span Rudder**: Illustrated below, with angles of 30° and 60° for the relative wind.

2. **Variables**:
   - **$L$**: Length of the rudder.
   - **$S_{R1}$ and $S_{R2}$**: Areas of the rudder sections.
   - **$L_1$ and $L_2$**: Distances from the center of gravity (c.g.) to the rudder sections.
   - **TDR (Total Drag Ratio)**: A key parameter that influences the effectiveness of the rudder.

3. **Equations**:
   - **URVC (Unsteady Rudder Velocity Coefficient)**: 
     $$ URVC = \frac{S_{R1} + S_{R2}}{S_{I}(2)} $$
   - **TDP (Total Drag Power)**: 
     $$ TDP = URVC \times TDR $$
   - **TDR Calculation**:
     $$ TDR = \frac{S_{F} L^2}{S_{I}(b/2)^2} $$

### Engineering Insights:
- The effectiveness of the rudder is significantly influenced by its span and the angle of the relative wind. Understanding these relationships helps in optimizing control surface design for improved aircraft stability and maneuverability.
- The calculations for URVC and TDP are crucial for determining the aerodynamic performance of the rudder under different flight conditions, which is essential for aircraft design and stability analysis. 

Overall, this figure emphasizes the importance of rudder geometry and relative wind direction in determining the control characteristics of an aircraft.
