This figure from "Airplane Stability and Control" illustrates the relationship between various oscillation modes and parameters affecting aircraft stability, specifically focusing on damping ratio, natural frequency, and real roots as functions of steady rolling velocity (\( p \), in radians per second).

### Top Panel: Damping Ratio
- **Axes**: The vertical axis represents the damping ratio, while the horizontal axis indicates steady rolling velocity (\( V \), in feet per second).
- **Curves**: The curves depict the damping ratios for lateral and longitudinal oscillations. As the velocity increases, the damping ratio for lateral oscillation initially increases, then stabilizes, while the longitudinal oscillation shows a decrease.

### Middle Panel: Natural Frequency
- **Axes**: The vertical axis represents natural frequency (in radians per second), and the horizontal axis is the same steady rolling velocity.
- **Curves**: The natural frequency for longitudinal oscillation increases with velocity, while the lateral oscillation frequency shows a more complex behavior, initially increasing and then leveling off.

### Bottom Panel: Real Roots
- **Axes**: The vertical axis shows real roots, indicating stability characteristics, while the horizontal axis again represents steady rolling velocity.
- **Curves**: The "Spiral Mode" and "Rolling Mode" curves indicate stability regions. The Spiral Mode remains positive, suggesting stable behavior, while the Rolling Mode dips below zero, indicating potential instability at certain velocities.

### Key Insight
The figure highlights how increasing steady rolling velocity affects the damping and natural frequencies of oscillation modes, which are crucial for understanding aircraft stability. Specifically, it shows that while lateral oscillations may become more stable with increased speed, longitudinal oscillations can exhibit complex behavior that may lead to instability in certain conditions. This understanding is vital for engineers designing stable aircraft.
